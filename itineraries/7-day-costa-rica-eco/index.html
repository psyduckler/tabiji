Navigation

Use Waze for driving ‚Äî better real-time road data than Google Maps in Costa Rica. Download Google Maps offline as backup for areas with no cell service. Costa Rica doesn't use street addresses ‚Äî directions are based on landmarks.

Buy a Kolbi SIM at the airport (‚Ç°5,000 for 4GB) or get an Airalo eSIM before arrival (~$10 for 5GB). Coverage is good on main routes; drops in mountains between Arenal and Manuel Antonio.

Family Tips

Costa Rica is incredibly kid-friendly. Most hotels offer family rooms or cabins. Car seats aren't always available with rentals ‚Äî bring your own if kids are under 4. Sunscreen and bug spray are essentials. Kids under 6 often enter national parks free.

Travel Insurance

Highly recommended for adventure activities (zip-lining, waterfall hikes). Costa Rica has good private healthcare but it's expensive without insurance. World Nomads or SafetyWing cover adventure sports. Verify your credit card's travel coverage first.

üåßÔ∏è Seasonal Guide: When to Visit Costa Rica

Costa Rica has two seasons: dry (verano) and green/rainy (invierno). Both have advantages.

Dry Season ‚Äî December to April

Peak tourist season. Sunny mornings, minimal rain, and everything is accessible. Christmas through Easter sees the highest prices and crowds. January‚ÄìFebruary is the sweet spot ‚Äî warm, dry, post-holiday crowds. Book accommodation 2‚Äì3 months ahead.

Green Season ‚Äî May to November

Mornings are usually sunny with afternoon/evening rain showers (1‚Äì3 hours). Landscapes are lush and green. Prices drop 20‚Äì40%, crowds thin dramatically. Wildlife is actually more active ‚Äî more birds, more frogs, and turtle nesting season on both coasts. Best value: May‚ÄìJune or November.

September‚ÄìOctober

Wettest months. Some roads become challenging. Pacific coast can see all-day rain. Caribbean coast (not on this itinerary) is paradoxically driest. Best avoided for this specific route unless you're flexible with plans.

üìù Costa Rica Tips That Actually Matter

Driving & Navigation

  • Use Waze, not just Google Maps. Waze has better real-time data for Costa Rica ‚Äî locals report road conditions, accidents, and police checkpoints. Google Maps is fine for general routing but Waze is superior for driving.
  • 4x4 is not optional. The road between La Fortuna and Manuel Antonio (inland route) has unpaved sections and occasionally river crossings in green season. Even in dry season, the steep hills require clearance a sedan can't handle.
  • Gas stations are full-service in Costa Rica ‚Äî attendants pump your gas. Tip ‚Ç°500‚Äì1,000. Stations can be sparse on rural routes ‚Äî fill up before leaving towns.
  • Addresses don't exist. Directions are given as "200 meters south of the church" or "in front of the old fig tree." Waze handles this; written directions from hotels may confuse you. When in doubt, call the hotel.
  • Never leave valuables in your car. Break-ins at trailheads and beaches are the #1 crime affecting tourists. Take everything with you or leave nothing visible.

Family-Specific Tips

  • Car seats: Required by law for children under 12 or under 145cm. Rental agencies offer them (~$10/day) but quality varies ‚Äî bringing your own is safest.
  • Bug spray: Essential in La Fortuna. Use DEET-based repellent or picaridin. Reapply after swimming. Mosquitoes are worst at dawn and dusk.
  • Pharmacies (farmacias) are well-stocked with kids' medications ‚Äî Tylenol, Benadryl, upset stomach remedies. No prescription needed for most things.
  • Pool vs. ocean: Most hotels in Manuel Antonio have pools ‚Äî great for afternoon downtime after park visits. Ocean currents can be strong on unprotected beaches.

Money & Costs

  • Costa Rica is not cheap compared to other Central American countries. Expect prices closer to US levels for tourist activities. Food at sodas (local restaurants) is much more affordable than tourist restaurants.
  • USD is accepted everywhere in tourist areas, but you'll get change in colones and often a worse exchange rate. Pay in colones when possible.
  • Tipping: Restaurants add a 10% service charge to bills. Small additional tips (5‚Äì10%) for great service are appreciated but not expected. Tip tour guides ‚Ç°3,000‚Äì5,000 per person.
  • Sodas (local restaurants) ‚Äî the best value. A full casado (rice, beans, plantain, salad, protein) costs ‚Ç°3,500‚Äì5,000 (~$7‚Äì10). Look for the handwritten "SODA" signs.

Wildlife Etiquette

  • Never feed wild animals. It's illegal in national parks and harmful to the animals. This includes the Manuel Antonio monkeys who will approach you ‚Äî don't encourage them.
  • Keep 3 meters distance from all wildlife. Use binoculars or zoom lenses instead of approaching.
  • Crocodiles: Don't swim in rivers near the coast, especially the T√°rcoles River near the Crocodile Bridge (Puente de Cocodrilos). Stop for photos from the bridge, but don't go near the water.

Packing Quick List

  • Reef-safe sunscreen ‚Äî required in national parks, better for the ocean
  • Water shoes ‚Äî for rocky beaches and waterfall swimming holes
  • Rain jacket ‚Äî it will rain at some point regardless of season
  • Binoculars ‚Äî game-changer for wildlife spotting
  • Dry bag ‚Äî protect electronics on boat trips and waterfall hikes
  • Insect repellent with DEET ‚Äî La Fortuna mosquitoes are relentless

Is 7 days enough for Costa Rica?

Seven days is perfect for a first trip covering two main regions ‚Äî Arenal volcano area and Manuel Antonio's beaches and wildlife. You'll experience rainforest, hot springs, wildlife, and Pacific coast without feeling rushed. Two weeks would let you add Monteverde cloud forest, Tortuguero, or the Osa Peninsula.

How much does a 7-day trip to Costa Rica cost?

For a mid-range family of four, budget $3,000‚Äì5,000 total excluding international flights. Costa Rica is more expensive than other Central American countries but offers excellent value for the experiences. Budget travelers can manage $1,200‚Äì2,000 for two people. The biggest expenses are accommodation, car rental, and national park activities. See the detailed budget breakdown above.

Do I need a 4x4 rental car in Costa Rica?

Highly recommended for this itinerary. Many roads between destinations are unpaved or have river crossings. The route from Arenal to Manuel Antonio includes rough sections. A 4x4 SUV costs $50‚Äì80/day including mandatory insurance. Book through Vamos Rent-a-Car or Adobe Rent-a-Car (local companies with better prices than international chains). Get full insurance ‚Äî Costa Rica's mandatory liability coverage is minimal.

Is Costa Rica safe for families?

Costa Rica is one of the safest countries in Central America and extremely family-friendly. Locals (Ticos) adore children. The main concerns are petty theft (don't leave valuables in your car at trailheads) and natural hazards (strong ocean currents at some beaches, never swim in rivers near the coast). Stick to well-traveled tourist routes and you'll have zero issues.

Should I use Waze or Google Maps for driving?

Use Waze for driving directions ‚Äî it has better real-time traffic and road condition data in Costa Rica, and locals actively report hazards. Download Google Maps offline as backup since cell coverage drops in mountainous areas between Arenal and Manuel Antonio. Some rental cars include GPS units but they're often outdated. Your phone with Waze is the best navigation tool.

When is the best time to visit Costa Rica?

Dry season (December‚ÄìApril) is peak tourist season with sunny skies and higher prices. Green season (May‚ÄìNovember) brings afternoon rain showers but lower prices, fewer crowds, and lush landscapes. The best value window is May‚ÄìJune or November ‚Äî shoulder months with manageable rain. Wildlife viewing is actually better in green season as animals are more active.
